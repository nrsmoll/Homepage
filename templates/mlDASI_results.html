Methods

The models were built on a dataset created by the METs Investigators for the assessement of functional capacity before major non-cardiac surgery.2 A range of machine learning and traditional biostatistical models were tested. The goal was to find the best fitting model with the lowest mean squared error and r2.

The models used to predict the VO2 Max are as follows:

  • Traditional Linear Regression
  • Kernal Ridge Regression
  • Random Forest Regression
  • Support Vector Regression

The models underwent hyper-parameter tuning where appropriate, with cross-validation (as re-sampling procedure) of the training and test datasets. The data was modelled in it's simplest form, with the yes/no answers to the DASI reduced to 0 and 1, instead of the suggested scores (which are simply weights). No transformation needed as the predictors were binary (0 and 1) as per the DASI questionnaire.

Models

The final model chosen to predict the VO2 Max and the AT was random forest regression (RFR). It predicted VO2 Max and AT better than all the other models tested, but resulting in a low R2 of 16. This was still far better than the Hlatky equation resulting in a very low R2 value of -1.8. Caution is advised when utilizing these results in clinical practice as the model accuracy is low. Nonetheless, the model presented here is far better than what has been previously used for the prediction of VO2 Max and AT, without utilizing formal cardiopulmonary exercise testing.
